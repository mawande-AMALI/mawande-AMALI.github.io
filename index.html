About

Building the data and delivery systems African cities need to govern better

At the intersection of urban governance, data systems, and public-sector delivery.

Mawande Ngidi is a public-sector delivery, urban governance, and data systems specialist working to strengthen how African cities make decisions, manage performance, and deliver on public priorities.

He currently leads the AMALI Data Programme at the African Centre for Cities, University of Cape Town — a multi-city technical assistance programme, in partnership with Bloomberg Philanthropies and Big Win Philanthropy, that supports African mayors, governors, and their teams to use data more effectively in delivering their legacy goals.

His work is deeply practical. Mawande supports cities not only to think about data, but to organise it, govern it, visualise it, and use it in real decision-making spaces. This means helping city teams move from paper-based records, fragmented spreadsheets, and siloed departmental data toward structured, reliable, decision-ready systems.

His approach is grounded in the belief that data is not an end in itself. Data becomes powerful when it is connected to leadership, routines, institutional accountability, and the everyday work of government. His work focuses on the full chain between information and action: clarifying goals, identifying the right indicators, understanding available datasets, strengthening data quality, building reporting tools, and creating regular performance conversations led by city leadership.

Mawande's professional focus is increasingly centred on the future of African urban data systems — including how cities can build the foundations needed for responsible use of AI, predictive analytics, remote sensing, and other emerging tools. His view is that AI strengthens, rather than replaces, the case for strong foundational urban data systems.

How I work

A practical, collaborative, implementation-focused approach

"In fast-growing African cities, better decisions depend on more than data. They depend on systems, routines, leadership, and the ability to turn evidence into action."

My work is practical, collaborative, and implementation-focused. I work with the systems cities already have, while helping them build toward stronger, more reliable, and more sustainable data practices.

The aim is not to create dependency on external experts. The aim is to build capability inside city halls — and to leave teams with routines, tools, and confidence that outlast any programme.

I work closely with mayors, governors, senior officials, technical departments, City Data Advisors, development partners, and academic institutions. The work spans site visits, workshops, peer learning, coaching, and technical assistance.

1
Start with the city's real priority

The work begins with the mayoral or city leadership priority — not a tool. The first step is understanding what the city is trying to achieve, why it matters, and what decisions leaders need to make.

2
Translate priorities into measurable goals

Broad ambitions are turned into clearer objectives, indicators, milestones, and delivery questions. This makes progress easier to track and easier to manage.

3
Understand the data landscape

Identifying what data exists, where it sits, who owns it, how it is collected, and whether it is reliable enough for decision-making. Building the inventory before building the dashboard.

4
Build practical reporting tools

The focus is on tools that city teams can actually use and maintain — dashboards, trackers, spreadsheets, templates, reporting forms, or lightweight data systems appropriate to the context.

5
Embed performance routines

Data only matters when it enters decision-making. Creating regular performance meetings where leaders use evidence to review progress, solve problems, and follow up.

6
Build capability, not dependency

The goal is to leave city teams stronger. Support is designed to build confidence, ownership, and internal routines that can continue beyond the programme.
